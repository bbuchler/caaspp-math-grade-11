Course Overview

What Students Are Working Toward

This course gives 11th grade students targeted practice with the math skills that show up repeatedly on CAASPP: equations, inequalities, linear relationships, slope/intercepts, function notation, modeling, and explaining mathematical reasoning. The course is organized into 20 lessons across four modules, with each module building from direct skill practice into real-world application.

Teacher takeaway: students are not just clicking through problems. They are watching, explaining, practicing, checking, and applying math in a way that produces work samples teachers can review.

Student Lesson Flow

  • Overview video: starts the lesson with the big idea.
  • Lesson vocabulary: previews key math terms before students use them.
  • Quick start: checks the core concept with a simple, low-pressure question.
  • Video questions: students answer while watching so the video is active, not passive.
  • Real-world task: students solve a scenario with supporting imagery.

Visual And Hands-On Supports

  • Infographics: show the main math idea in a visual form students can scan.
  • Kinesthetic practice: hands-on tasks, such as the balance/undo board, ask students to choose and move the inverse operation instead of only reading steps.
  • Worked examples: model the process, common mistakes, and how to check an answer.
  • Accessible wording: lesson text should stay clear and student-friendly without watering down the math vocabulary.

Practice And Extension

  • Retryable practice: students can update practice answers as they learn.
  • Exit check: final lesson evidence is one attempt and should show whether the core skill stuck.
  • Finish line: students see what they learned and whether they are ready to move on.
  • Extension activities: optional bonus problems push students into a slightly harder or more creative version of the skill.
Grading

How Scores And Work Samples Work

Practice questions save as students go, so a student can leave midway through a lesson and return later. Practice items can be updated, and the newest answer replaces the previous score.

Exit check questions are one attempt because they are the lesson's final evidence. A student can pass the lesson with an overall score of 70% or higher, but a low exit check still flags the student for teacher follow-up.

Teacher work samples show the student name, course, lesson number/title, last activity, total score, percent, follow-up status, answers, feedback, and teacher override controls. Teachers can override individual scores and regrade individual AI-scored responses when needed.

AI Support

Study Buddy And Teacher Insights

Study Buddy: the student-facing AI helper should use the current lesson, visible page context, student answer, answer key, and rubric so it can help with the exact math on screen.

AI grading: written explanations receive partial credit and feedback. Objective math items still use deterministic checks when possible, because exact answers should not depend on a language model.

Teacher insights: the dashboard flags students who need follow-up and summarizes class patterns from saved responses. Over time, this can support small groups, remediation lessons, and targeted review before the next module.

Teacher Workflow

Using The Gradebook

The gradebook shows one row per student and one column per lesson. Teachers can filter by module, click a lesson score to open the work sample, reset passwords from student rows, print work samples, and export scores.

The detail panel focuses on the selected lesson rather than trying to show every section from all 20 lessons at once. That keeps the dashboard usable for a full class while still making individual lesson evidence easy to inspect.
